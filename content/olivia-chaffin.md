# Olivia Chaffin's Story

Olivia Chaffin was one of the best cookie sellers in her Girl Scout troop in Jonesborough, Tennessee. She sold over 600 boxes and earned a badge for it. For her, Girl Scouts was about the idea that girls could actually do good in the world.

When Olivia heard that palm oil production was linked to rainforest loss in places like Indonesia and Malaysia, she started reading the ingredients on her own cookie boxes. Sure enough, palm oil was right there on the label.

Olivia noticed something else, too: a little green tree logo and the words "certified sustainable." At first that was reassuring — it sounded like the palm oil in her cookies wasn't the kind that hurt rainforests or orangutans.

Then she looked closer and saw the word "mixed" on the label. When she looked it up, she learned something surprising. "Mixed" meant that sustainable palm oil had been blended with palm oil from other sources somewhere in the supply chain, so you couldn't trace any specific box back to any specific farm. It was more complicated than she had expected, and she wanted to understand it better.

So Olivia started asking questions. She asked her troop, she researched certifications, she read about workers and farms across the world. The more she learned, the more she realized how many people, places, and choices go into a single cookie.

Olivia is about 20 now. She stopped selling cookies at 14, and the questions she had been asking eventually turned into a petition that was covered by national news. It started a real conversation about transparency in food supply chains — one that is still pushing the industry to be more open about where ingredients come from.

This site exists because of the example she set. Every Scout at a cookie booth deserves to be able to answer the simple question a customer might ask: *where does this come from?* Understanding your own product isn't about tearing anything down — it's about standing behind what you sell with confidence, and knowing the full story of what's in the box.
