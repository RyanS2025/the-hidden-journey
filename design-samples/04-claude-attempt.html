1. Palm oil farming Indonesia & Malaysia
85% of global palm oil comes from plantations in Southeast Asia. Girl Scout Cookies use RSPO Mass Balance certified oil — sustainable blended with conventional sources, not fully traceable.
An AP investigation linked palm oil supplying cookie bakers to child labor. A 10-year-old named Ima was pulled from school to work 12-hour days on a plantation connected to the supply chain.
1.8 kg CO₂
2. Cocoa sourcing Côte d'Ivoire & Ghana
Little Brownie Bakers is owned by Ferrero (Italy), which signed the Cocoa and Forest Initiative to end deforestation and support sustainable cocoa communities in West Africa.
0.9 kg CO₂
3. Domestic ingredients U.S. Midwest
Wheat flour, sugar, and soybean oil sourced from American farms. Cookies contain GMOs from corn, soybeans, canola, and sugar beets.
0.4 kg CO₂
4. Baking Louisville, KY or North Sioux City, SD
Two licensed bakeries: Little Brownie Bakers (owned by Italian Ferrero — yes, the Nutella company) and ABC Bakers (owned by Canadian Weston Foods). Neither is American-owned.
The cookies Girl Scouts sell door-to-door are ultimately produced by an Italian and a Canadian corporation.
0.6 kg CO₂
5. Packaging Various U.S. locations
Boxes are 100% recycled paperboard. No BPA or PFAS chemicals. Some varieties shifted to plastic film — less weight, less fuel, but not widely recyclable.
0.3 kg CO₂
6. Your neighborhood Door-to-door or booth sales
From bakeries to 112 regional councils, then to troop leaders, then to you. Nearly 200 million boxes generate ~$800 million annually. The largest girl-led business in the world.
0.5 kg CO₂
The story behind the story
One Girl Scout read her cookie box and changed the conversation.
At age 11, Olivia Chaffin was a top cookie seller in Troop 543 in Jonesborough, Tennessee. Then she spotted the word "mixed" next to the RSPO palm oil label on her Thin Mints box. She researched what it meant, learned that sustainable oil was blended with conventional sources linked to deforestation and child labor — and stopped selling.

Her Change.org petition gathered 23,000+ signatures. Troops nationwide boycotted cookie sales in solidarity. An AP investigation later confirmed the connection between Girl Scout Cookie supply chains and child labor on Indonesian palm plantations.
"I thought Girl Scouts was supposed to be about making the world a better place. But this isn't at all making the world better."

— Olivia Chaffin, age 14

Why this matters
What can you do with this knowledge?
Understanding supply chains is the first step toward changing them.
🌍
Environment
Global shipping emits 1 billion tonnes of CO₂ annually — 3% of all emissions. Where your product comes from determines its footprint.
🤝
Labor rights
Tens of thousands of children work on palm oil plantations. Knowing the supply chain means knowing who's affected.
🔍
Transparency
Labels like "sustainable" and "mixed" mean very different things. One word on a cookie box started a national conversation.
💡
Consumer power
Companies change when consumers ask questions. Olivia Chaffin was 11 when she started asking. You can start now.
